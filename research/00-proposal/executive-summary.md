# Executive Summary & Presentation — PhD Research Program

**Working title:** *A Domain-Invariant Maturity Meta-Model for Adaptive Systems:
Development, Cross-Domain Transfer, and Predictive Validity*

*One-page summary for supervisors, committees, and stakeholders, followed by a
slide-by-slide presentation outline.*

---

## Executive Summary

**The problem.** Organizations and managed socio-technical systems are universally
described as "maturing" from raw data toward intelligent, self-adapting behavior — yet
the stages are defined ad hoc and per industry. Established maturity models (CMMI,
data-maturity frameworks) are **single-domain, conceptually derived, and descriptive**.
None has been shown to be simultaneously **domain-invariant, empirically validated, and
predictive of outcomes**.

**The contribution.** This research develops and tests an **11-level maturity
meta-model of adaptive capability** (Reality → Observation → Data → Knowledge →
Intelligence → Wisdom → Purpose → Action → Learning → Adaptation → Evolution),
grounded in the DIKW hierarchy and the cybernetic/organizational-learning control loop.
The central question:

> *Can one fixed structure describe — and predict adaptation outcomes in — multiple
> unrelated domains without per-domain modification?*

**The approach (four studies).**
1. Systematic review establishing the gap.
2. Instrument development & validation (reliability, construct validity).
3. Cross-domain transfer test across ≥3 unrelated domains (agriculture, supply chain,
   clinical pathways) — **H1**.
4. Predictive-validity test: maturity vs. adaptation outcomes against a baseline — **H2**.

**Why it matters.** A validated, transferable maturity science would give organizations
a shared, measurable, *predictive* language for adaptive capability — and underwrite a
new class of decision-intelligence tooling. It also contributes a rare empirical kernel
to the broader science of complex adaptive systems.

**What makes it credible.** The program is **designed to be proven wrong**: a
pre-registered null hypothesis (any domain needing structural edits refutes invariance),
falsifiable predictions, and a strict scope boundary that excludes metaphysical
over-generalization.

**Feasibility.** A working instrument (rubric v1.1), a two-domain pilot, a theory draft,
a review protocol, and a dataset scan already exist — de-risking the first two years.

**One-liner.** *Turn "organizational maturity" from an industry buzzword into a
measurable, cross-domain, predictive science — and be willing to prove it doesn't
generalize.*

## Presentation Outline (≈12 slides)

1. **Title** — title, candidate, proposed supervisor/program.
2. **The hook** — every industry has a "maturity model"; none is validated *across*
   industries or shown to *predict* anything.
3. **The gap** — the 2×2 (single/cross-domain × descriptive/predictive); the
   bottom-right cell is empty. *That cell is the thesis.*
4. **Research question & hypotheses** — RQ; H1 transfer; H2 prediction; H0 falsifier.
5. **The model** — the 11-level ladder, one clean diagram.
6. **Theory** — DIKW + cybernetics/learning loop; L8→L9→L10 = single/double/deutero
   learning (the strongest defense).
7. **Method** — rubric + survey instrument; 3 domains; reliability + predictive design.
8. **Four studies** — review → instrument → transfer → prediction, as a pipeline.
9. **Falsifiability** — the pre-registered null; why "no" is still a result.
10. **Significance** — science contribution + applied (decision-intelligence) angle.
11. **Feasibility & timeline** — preliminary work done; 4-year plan; ethics early.
12. **Scope discipline** — what it is (a bounded, provable contribution) and what it is
    *not* (a universal theory of everything). Close on the one-liner.

### Speaker notes — the three things a committee must remember
- There is a **specific, provable gap** (the empty cell).
- The work **can fail** (pre-registered H0) — this is a feature.
- The candidate **knows the difference** between the thesis and the grand vision.
