For most of my career as an enterprise architect and AI strategy leader, I have built
systems meant to help organizations sense, decide, learn, and adapt. Across industries
I kept meeting the same artifact: a "maturity model" — for data, for digital, for
process — used to tell an organization how advanced it is and what to do next. And I
kept noticing the same flaw: these models are asserted, not tested. They are
single-industry, conceptually derived, and descriptive. None could tell me whether a
higher "maturity" score actually *predicted* that an organization would adapt better
to a shock — and none had been shown to hold across unrelated domains. I want to spend
a PhD turning that gap into science.

My research question is deliberately narrow and falsifiable: **can a single,
domain-invariant model of adaptive capability both describe and predict adaptation
outcomes across unrelated domains, without per-domain modification?** I propose an
11-level meta-model — from observation and data through knowledge, intelligence,
wisdom, and on to learning, adaptation, and evolution — grounded not in intuition but
in established theory: the DIKW hierarchy and the cybernetic/organizational-learning
control loop, where the top levels map onto Argyris and Schön's single-, double-, and
deutero-learning. I will test it through four studies: a systematic review to establish
the gap, instrument development and validation, a cross-domain transfer test across
agriculture, supply chain, and clinical pathways, and a predictive-validity test
against domain-specific baselines. Crucially, I have built in a pre-registered null
hypothesis: if any domain requires changing the model's structure, the invariance claim
is refuted. I am as interested in disproving the model as confirming it.

This question grew directly out of practice. In exploring how a single "evolution
engine" might describe systems from agriculture to enterprises, I developed and
stress-tested a conceptual framework across dozens of iterations — and learned its most
important lesson the hard way: the parts that reached for a "theory of everything" were
unfalsifiable and worthless, while the grounded core (a measurable maturity ladder
applied to real systems) was genuinely promising. That experience taught me the
discipline this proposal embodies: separate the falsifiable engineering core from the
grand vision, and let only the former count as a thesis. I have since produced a working
measurement instrument, a two-domain pilot, a theoretical grounding, and a review
protocol — the scaffolding of the program — which I am eager to subject to proper
academic rigor and supervision.

My preparation is unusual in a useful way. As a practitioner I bring real enterprise
context, the systems-engineering and AI background to build and instrument the model,
and access to organizational settings where it can be tested. What I am seeking from
the PhD is the complementary half: methodological rigor in design science, psychometric
validation, causal and predictive analysis, and the philosophy of science needed to
defend a falsifiable theory. I am committed to the unglamorous core of research —
reliable measurement, pre-registration, honest reporting of negative results — because
that is precisely what this over-hyped problem area lacks.

My longer-term goal is to help found a credible, cumulative line of work on the science
of adaptation — and, eventually, to inform a new class of decision-support tools that
give organizations a validated, predictive language for their own adaptive capacity.
But I hold that vision as a north star, not a deliverable. The thesis itself will do one
thing well: establish whether adaptive capability has a measurable, transferable,
predictive structure — and say so honestly, whichever way the evidence falls. That is
the contribution I want to make, and [program] is where I want to make it.
